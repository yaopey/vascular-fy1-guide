Carotid Endarterectomy (CEA) removes plaque at the common carotid artery (CCA) bifurcation to reduce the risk of stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA). Patients are admitted on the day or transferred from the stroke ward, and are typically discharged day 1–2 post-op.

⚠️
Pre-op
Do not stop single or dual antiplatelet therapy before surgery. All patients fasted 6 hours prior.
🩺
Post-op complications — escalate urgently
  • Stroke: urgent CT brain, inform on-call registrar immediately
  • Neck haematoma + stridor: airway emergency — anaesthetics + escalate now
  • BP >180mmHg: risk of cerebral hyperperfusion syndrome
  • BP <100mmHg: risk of thrombosis at surgical site
  • Continue antiplatelet therapy post-op
📅
Follow-up
1 week
Telephone follow-up with vascular specialist nurses
6–8 weeks
Consultant outpatient review
Stroke follow-up
Check with referring stroke consultant if required

Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA) can be repaired via open surgery or Endovascular Aneurysm Repair (EVAR). Open repair patients go to ICU post-op before stepping down to the ward. EVAR patients go directly to the ward post-op.

📏
Size thresholds
≥5.5cm
Intervene
3–5.4cm
US surveillance
Pre-op medications: Withhold anticoagulation. Stop clopidogrel 7 days before. Aspirin and prophylactic LMWH may continue.

10% of AAA patients also have popliteal artery aneurysm (PAA). Symptomatic/large PAA needs intervention; smaller ones need US surveillance.

📆
Open AAA — complication timeline
Day 1
Post-op ileus, renal failure
Days 2–4
Chest infection, electrolyte imbalance, renal failure
Days 5–8
Wound infection, chest/UTI, PE
Ileus beyond day 5
CT abdomen-pelvis with oral gastrografin to exclude mechanical obstruction
🔬
EVAR post-op
  • Goes directly to the ward post-op (not ICU)
  • Typically discharged within a few days
  • Outpatient surveillance CTA in one month

The most common patients you'll see on the ward. Conduit: long saphenous/arm vein or prosthetic graft.

🔀
Types of bypass
Femoro-popliteal (AK/BK) Femoro-distal (crural) Femoro-femoral crossover Ipsi/contralateral ilio-femoral Common femoral endarterectomy
These patients have systemic atherosclerosis. Consider brain, heart, and kidney involvement when assessing them.
🩺
Post-op care
  • All bypasses need anti-platelet or anti-coagulation — read post-op instructions
  • Vein bypasses: US surveillance needed — consultant to arrange; document on discharge
  • Watch for: wound bleeding, reperfusion syndrome, leg swelling, wound infection
  • Graft thrombosis → acute limb ischaemia — escalate immediately if concerned about perfusion
  • Failed bypass may require major lower limb amputation

Offered for end-stage atherosclerosis with no further revascularisation options. Level depends on blood supply, tissue quality, and ambulation potential.

📍
Amputation levels
Above knee Through knee Below knee Transmetatarsal/forefoot Toe/Ray
🩺
Post-op care
  • Check post-op instructions; check bloods next day (especially Hb)
  • Phantom limb pain: ensure opiate analgesia ± gabapentin/pregabalin/amitriptyline. Pain team reviews on ward
  • Major amputation wound reviewed several days post-op — watch for infection, skin necrosis, stump ischaemia
  • Wound problems may require return to theatre for stump revision
  • Toe amputation: chase tissue/bone culture; discuss antibiotics with microbiology (MDT: Wed 1pm, radiology seminar room)
  • Osteomyelitis: 6 weeks IV antibiotics via OPAT — organise access line

Usually poorly controlled diabetes. Requires debridement/amputation, revascularisation, antibiotics, and podiatry for protective footwear.

✅
Clerking checklist
  • PMH including smoking history and previous vascular interventions
  • Document lower limb pulses and sensation
  • Examine web spaces between toes
  • Check all bloods including HbA1c
  • If pyrexic: blood cultures before starting antibiotics
  • Foot X-ray for ?osteomyelitis
  • Wound swab
  • Start IV FMG unless previous culture/sensitivity results available
  • Withhold anticoagulation if likely surgical — discuss with registrar
  • If in doubt, keep fasted until senior review
  • Insulin sliding scale if high BM on admission

Access via common femoral artery. Wires, catheters, and balloon used to treat stenotic/occlusive disease. Done in the interventional radiology suite.

⚠️
Complications
Puncture-related: bruising, bleeding, haematoma, pseudoaneurysm
Arterial: distal embolisation and dissection → acute limb ischaemia
General: contrast-induced nephropathy, reperfusion syndrome, unresolved symptoms
🩺
Post-procedure care
  • Read procedural notes and post-procedural care instructions
  • Resume medications that were withheld before the procedure
  • Examine puncture site for haematoma/pseudoaneurysm; check limb perfusion
  • Post-procedural U&Es

Vascular patients have systemic atherosclerosis — renal, cardiac, and cerebrovascular involvement is common.

🚨
Acute bleed on ward
Remove all dressings → identify source → direct pressure (finger/hand) or tourniquet proximal to bleeding point → shout for help immediately.
📋
Key rules
4
All patients: Clopidogrel 75mg OD + Atorvastatin 80mg unless contraindicated. On warfarin/DOAC → switch to therapeutic LMWH on admission, resume prior to discharge.
5
Omit ACE-i/ARBs and metformin before any IV/IA contrast procedure (angioplasty, EVAR, CT angiogram).
6
Contrast nephropathy: raised creatinine after day 3. Interpret relative to muscle mass — creatinine 110 in a 45kg elderly woman = renal failure.
7
All pre-op patients need: FBC, UE, LFT, clotting, 2× group & save, ECG — completed before day of surgery.
8
Don't stop antiplatelets or prophylactic LMWH pre-op. Stop therapeutic LMWH 12h before; DOAC 48h before. Target INR <1.5 for warfarin.
10
All patients on prophylactic LMWH unless specified otherwise.
11
All patients with groin wound: 1-week post-discharge wound check in vascular lab + 6–8 week outpatient follow-up. Document both in discharge summary.
13
Most vascular patients do not need mechanical VTE prophylaxis (TED stockings) — especially those with PVD.
14
Post-op heparin: use treatment-dose LMWH, not IV infusion. Monitor platelets for HIT.
